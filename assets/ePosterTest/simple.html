The Shock-Driven Outflows of Tidal Disruption Events

The first light from tidal disruption events, and an optical emission mechanism
Alexander Dittmann, University of Maryland

Tidal disruption events (TDEs) begin when a star is torn apart by the tidal forces from a supermassive black hole (SMBH). Approximately half of the star will remain bound, and come back for another round...
From Rees (1988).

TDEs are farily interesting because they might help us identify lower-mass black holes. Most quasi-steadily accreting black holes have masses closer to ~billions of solar masses. However, SMBHs with masses greater than about ~40 million times solar will instead be swallowed whole. These TDEs might light up otherwise quiescent galaxies, offering us a window into the lower-mass portion of the supermassive black hole populaiton.

General relativistic GR precession causes the falling-back gas to hit itself. This should emit light and help the returning material form a disk. As I'll show later, this can also drive outflows if the shock is strong enough.
From Piran et al. (2015), adapted from Shiokawa et al. (2015).

Notably, these shocks might be strong enough to unbind a significant amount of material....
